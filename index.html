I grew up in Pune, India, drawn to computers early — more interested in how games and machines worked than almost anything else. When it came time to choose a stream after school, Science was the obvious call over Arts or Commerce; I liked maths, but not enough to give up computers for it. That pull carried straight through into a BSc in Computer Science.

I could have started a software career in India straight after graduating. Instead, I chose to go further — and the timing lined up with the moment large language models stopped being a research curiosity and became something everyone was suddenly using. Watching tools like ChatGPT and Claude go from novelty to genuinely useful, in real time, is what convinced me AI was where I wanted to build a career, not just study it.

That decision took me to Aston University in Birmingham for an MSc in Artificial Intelligence — a long way from home. The hardest part was never the coursework; it was the distance from my family, further than I'd ever lived from them before. Sticking with it anyway taught me more about persistence than any single module did, and it's that same mindset I bring to finishing what I start.

Now I'm focused on turning that into a full-time role in AI — and building a portfolio that actually shows the work, not just lists it.

~/dissertation/deepfake-detection MSc Dissertation · Aston University · Supervised by Prof. Nitin Naik

Multimodal Deepfake Detection for Audio & Video

Most deepfake detectors judge a clip on video alone, or audio alone — which means a fake with convincing visuals but flawed audio (or vice versa) can slip through. This project builds a pipeline that weighs both signals together, then tests whether that actually helps once the clips get harder to classify.

video frames → face crop → ResNet-18
＋

→

→
real / fake

Trained with a two-stage transfer-learning schedule — the ResNet-18 backbone frozen while the new head stabilises, then fully unfrozen for fine-tuning at a lower learning rate — with class-weighted loss to handle imbalanced real/fake samples. Every experiment ran on CPU only. The single most important design choice was subject-disjoint splitting: identities in the test set never appear in training, so the model has to learn genuine deepfake artefacts rather than memorising faces.
